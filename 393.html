A study is proposed to ascertain whether childhood obesity in girls increases the risk of polycystic ovarian syndrome. Which of the following study designs would be be most appropriate and would provide the most robust evidence regarding the link between the two?

Cohort study

The correct answer is Cohort study. A cohort study is ideal for determining the relationship between childhood obesity and the subsequent development of polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS) because it follows participants over time, allowing the observation of incidence. This type of study is prospective, starting with a group of girls with and without obesity, and tracking them into the future to see who develops PCOS. The prospective nature of the study provides strong evidence for temporal relationships, which is crucial when investigating risk factors for disease development. In clinical practice, cohort studies help establish robust links between risk factors (like obesity) and conditions (like PCOS), which can influence preventive strategies in childhood to reduce later life risks.

Case-control study is retrospective, starting with girls who already have PCOS and looking back to determine if they were obese as children. While case-control studies are useful for rare conditions, PCOS is reasonably common, making a cohort study more appropriate. Case-control studies are also prone to bias, particularly recall bias, and since the temporal sequence of childhood obesity leading to PCOS is critical, a case-control study would not be the most robust design for this purpose.

Cross-over trial is not suitable in this context. Cross-over trials are mainly used for interventions where participants can act as their own control by switching between treatments. This design is impractical for studying the long-term association between a risk factor (childhood obesity) and a non-reversible outcome (development of PCOS), as childhood obesity cannot be 'crossed over' between subjects.

Randomised controlled trial (RCT) could provide strong evidence, but it is not feasible or ethical in this scenario. Randomising girls to become obese or not in childhood in order to study PCOS risk would raise significant ethical concerns. Furthermore, RCTs are more appropriate for testing the effects of interventions rather than natural exposure outcomes such as obesity.

Cross-sectional survey is inappropriate here because it provides a snapshot at one point in time. It may show an association between childhood obesity and PCOS, but it cannot determine the temporal relationship or causality since both variables are assessed simultaneously. This limits its value in studying long-term risk factors for disease.
