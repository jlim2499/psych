What is the most appropriate interpretation of this diagram?


A lack of small studies, which may show a negative (higher risk ratio) result, may reflect publication bias

Explanation for the Correct Answer:

A lack of small studies, which may show a negative (higher risk ratio) result, may reflect publication bias: A funnel plot is a scatter plot of the treatment effects estimated from individual studies against some measure of each study's size or precision. In the absence of bias, the plot resembles a symmetrical inverted funnel because the larger studies will tend to cluster around the mean effect, while smaller studies will be spread on both sides due to chance variability. If there is a gap in the funnel plot where there should be small studies reporting negative or null results (higher risk ratios), this may indicate publication bias, as it suggests that smaller studies showing less effect or negative results are missing from the literature, possibly because they are less likely to be published.

Explanation for the Incorrect Answers:

There is no publication bias: This is incorrect if the funnel plot shows asymmetry, which would suggest that there is potentially some degree of publication bias.

The asymmetry in the plot cannot have arisen by chance: This statement cannot be confirmed without statistical testing. Asymmetry can suggest bias but does not prove it.

Publication bias will have resulted from the limited publication of larger sized studies which demonstrate a protective effect from taking aspirin: If the funnel plot showed missing studies at the larger end, this could be a valid interpretation. However, it is usually the smaller studies that are underreported.

Publication bias will have resulted from the over publication of larger sized studies which demonstrate a protective effect from taking aspirin: Over-representation of larger studies would not typically indicate publication bias, as larger studies are expected to be published regardless of their results. Publication bias more commonly involves a lack of published smaller studies.

It's important to note that while funnel plots are a useful tool for visualising potential publication bias, they cannot prove its existence. Statistical tests and careful examination of the context of the studies are necessary for a definitive conclusion.
