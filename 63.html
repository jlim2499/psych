Which of the following test properties refers to the probability that a person who has the disease will test positive?

Sensitivity

The correct answer is Sensitivity. Sensitivity refers to the probability that a person who has the disease will test positive — in other words, the proportion of true positives among all those who actually have the condition. A test with high sensitivity is especially valuable for screening, as it minimises false negatives. In psychiatry, for example, a highly sensitive screening tool for postnatal depression ensures that most women with the condition are identified, even if a few false positives occur. This is crucial for early intervention in high-risk groups where missing a case could have serious consequences.

Precision is incorrect. In the context of diagnostic testing, 'precision' is not a standard term used in clinical epidemiology. It may refer to the reproducibility or reliability of measurements, but it is not synonymous with sensitivity, specificity, or predictive values.

Negative predictive value is incorrect. Negative predictive value (NPV) refers to the probability that a person with a negative test result truly does not have the disease. It depends on both the test's specificity and the prevalence of disease in the population.

Specificity is incorrect. Specificity is the probability that a person without the disease will test negative — that is, the proportion of true negatives among those who are actually disease-free. It is the complementary property to sensitivity and is important when confirming diagnoses or avoiding overdiagnosis.

Positive predictive value is incorrect. Positive predictive value (PPV) is the probability that someone with a positive test result truly has the disease. Unlike sensitivity, which is an inherent property of the test, PPV varies with disease prevalence in the population being tested.
