Clock drawing test


The clock drawing test is used as a quick screening test for cognitive dysfunction.

The patient is asked to draw a clock on a piece of paper, putting the numbers on the clock and drawing on the clock hands in the position of 10 minutes past 11.

The test examines a range of different cognitive functions simultaneously, including:-

  • Visuospatial ability
  • Motor function
  • Attention
  • Comprehension

There are several scoring systems available, but one of the most commonly used is a 5-point system.

Scoring of the CDT:

  • Clock circle:
    • The patient draws a complete circle (1 point).
    • A non-circular or poorly drawn clock shape gets no point.
  • Numbers correctly placed:
    • All 12 numbers are present and correctly placed in the circle (1 point).
    • Missing or mispositioned numbers receive no point.
  • Numbers in correct sequence:
    • The numbers are in the correct order, with appropriate spacing (1 point).
    • If the numbers are jumbled or in the wrong sequence, no point is given.
  • Clock hands:
    • The clock has two hands (1 point).
    • A correct clock will have both an hour hand and a minute hand. If only one hand is drawn or they are not differentiated, no point is given.
  • Correct time (10 past 11):
    • The clock hands are set to the correct time (1 point).
    • Any other time or incorrect placement of the hands will result in no point for this category.

Total Score:

The maximum score on the CDT is 5 points, with each of the five components earning 1 point.

Interpretation:

A score of 5 typically indicates normal cognitive function.

A score of 4 or lower suggests the possibility of cognitive impairment, with the types of errors made helping to distinguish between different causes, such as dementia or motor-related conditions like Parkinson's disease.

Some versions of the CDT use a 10-point scoring system, which breaks down the task into more detailed components (e.g., separating the placement of the numbers into quadrants or evaluating the relative lengths of the hands), but the 5-point system remains widely used due to its simplicity and clinical relevance.
