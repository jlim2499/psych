Which test measure would be most helpful in order to reassure a patient who has received a negative test result?

Negative predictive value

The correct answer is Negative predictive value. The negative predictive value (NPV) of a test refers to the probability that a person who receives a negative test result truly does not have the disease. In other words, it's the likelihood that a negative result is, in fact, true. Thus, if you want to reassure a patient who has received a negative test result, knowing the NPV of the test would be most helpful.

The Positive predictive value, on the other hand, indicates how likely it is that a person who received a positive test result truly has the disease. It wouldn't be as useful in this scenario because we are looking at patients with negative results.

Specificity is another important measure but it refers to how accurately the test identifies those without the disease (true negatives). While high specificity can lead to high NPV, they are not interchangeable and specificity alone cannot provide reassurance for an individual patient with a negative test result.

The term Sensitivity describes how accurately the test identifies those with the disease (true positives). A highly sensitive test means that there are few false negatives, and thus might seem relevant here. However, sensitivity does not take into account false positives - therefore it doesn't directly help us understand how reliable our particular negative result is.

While precision is indeed an important quality in diagnostic testing, it does not inform us about whether or not an individual's specific negative result can be trusted as truly indicating absence of disease.
