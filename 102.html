The external validity of a study refers to which of the following?

The degree to which the conclusions in a study would hold for other persons in other places and at other times

Explanation for the Correct Answer:

The degree to which the conclusions in a study would hold for other persons in other places and at other times is the correct answer because external validity is concerned with the applicability of the study findings to contexts beyond the study itself.

Explanation for the Incorrect Answers:

The extent to which an experiment, test, or any measuring procedure yields the same result on repeated trials refers to reliability, not external validity.

The confidence that we can place in the cause and effect relationship in a study describes internal validity, which is concerned with the study's methodological soundness and the precision of the cause-effect deductions within the study.

The extent to which a test measures the construct it aims to is about construct validity, meaning how accurately a test measures the theoretical concept it is intended to measure.

The extent to which a test or measure assesses the full content of a subject or area describes content validity, which pertains to the extent to which a test covers the representative breadth of the subject matter.
