Which of the following is the correct definition of the standardised mortality ratio?

The ratio of observed to expected mortality in a sample population

The correct answer is The ratio of observed to expected mortality in a sample population. The Standardised Mortality Ratio (SMR) is a comparison between the actual number of deaths observed in a sample population and the number of deaths expected based on age- and sex-specific rates in a standard population. This allows for meaningful comparisons across different populations by accounting for demographic differences.

The additional mortality in a study group compared to a reference group: This is incorrect because it suggests an absolute difference in mortality rather than a ratio. The SMR is based on a ratio, not a direct comparison of additional deaths.

The change in mortality rates over a specific time period: This option refers to temporal changes in mortality, which is not the focus of the SMR. The SMR compares observed and expected deaths within a fixed study period, without focusing on trends over time.

The proportion of deaths in a study group over the total population: This describes a simple proportion or rate of deaths within a group, without reference to expected mortality or adjustments for factors such as age or sex, which are essential in the SMR calculation. The SMR specifically standardises for these factors.

The typical mortality based on long-term trends: This refers to general mortality patterns over time, but the SMR deals specifically with comparing observed deaths to expected deaths in a study population, not long-term trends or averages.

Clinically, the SMR is valuable when comparing mortality outcomes in different populations, such as assessing whether a specific hospital or population has a higher or lower than expected mortality rate compared to the general population. For example, a hospital with an SMR greater than 1 would be experiencing more deaths than expected, potentially indicating an issue with patient care or other factors affecting outcomes.
