According to traditional approaches, which of the following is considered to be the highest level of evidence?

RCTs with non-definitive results

The correct answer is RCTs with non-definitive results. The traditional hierarchy of evidence places Randomised Controlled Trials (RCTs) at the top due to their robust methodology, which minimises bias and confounding factors. Even if an RCT has non-definitive results, it still provides a higher level of evidence than other study designs because its design allows for cause-and-effect relationships to be established.

Cohort studies are observational in nature and although they can provide useful information about the natural history of disease and risk factors, they lack the randomisation that is key in RCTs. Therefore, while cohort studies can suggest associations between risk factors and outcomes, they cannot definitively prove causation due to potential confounding variables.

Case-control studies, like cohort studies, are observational. They start with an outcome (e.g., disease) and look backwards to find associated exposures or risk factors. This retrospective approach means that recall bias is a significant issue, as well as selection bias for cases and controls. Furthermore, case-control studies only provide odds ratios which do not directly estimate risk or rate ratios like cohort studies or RCTs do.

Cross sectional surveys measure both exposure and outcome at a single point in time. They are often used for estimating prevalence but cannot determine causality because there's no temporal relationship established between exposure and outcome.

Finally, expert opinion ranks lowest on the evidence hierarchy. While expert opinion can be valuable in areas where empirical evidence is lacking or inconclusive, it's important to remember that it can be influenced by individual biases or conflicts of interest.

In conclusion, according to traditional approaches, RCTs even with non-definitive results offer the highest level of evidence because they allow for causal relationships to be established through randomisation and control groups. Other types of studies have various limitations that reduce their ability to provide definitive evidence of causation.
