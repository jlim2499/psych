Dissociative seizures


Non-epileptic seizures are abnormal paroxysmal psychic, sensory and/or motor manifestations which resemble (at least in part) to epileptic seizures but are not related to abnormal epileptiform discharges (electrical activity in the brain).

This can be from physical causes such as hypoglycaemia or cardiac dysfunction. More commonly however the non-epileptic seizure results from mental or emotional processes in which case it is referred to as a dissociative seizure (previously termed 'pseudo seizure' or 'functional seizures').

Note: Dissociative seizure are not the same as factitious seizures, which are voluntary.
Dissociative seizures can consist of a fall (usually safe), a gesticulation (or, on the contrary, a frozen posture), pseudo-clonus of the limbs, ocular revulsion with or without eye blinking, or simply an eye closure with a subsequent non-responsive attitude.

Dissociative seizures are more common in females and tend to have an onset in late adolescence.

The following features are helpful when trying to distinguish between true seizures and pseudo seizures (Mellers, 2005):


Serum prolactin rises to concentrations greater than 500 IU/ml in over 90% of patients after a tonic-clonic seizure and 60% of patients after a complex partial seizure. Simple partial seizures are not followed by a rise in serum prolactin and prolactin concentrations may be normal after prolonged status epilepticus. Blood must be taken between 20 and 30 minutes after the seizure and compared with a baseline sample. The test is, however, used less and less in specialist centres as false positive results have now been found in syncope and dissociative seizures (Mellers, 2005).

TREATMENT:

A 2014 Cochrane Review on psychological and behavioural treatments concluded that there is little reliable evidence to support the use of any treatment, including cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT), for psychogenic nonepileptic seizures.

Although data are limited, it is noteworthy that CBT is the only psychotherapeutic intervention studied as a treatment for psychogenic nonepileptic seizures in randomized controlled trials and, as a result, has the highest level of efficacy evidence (Kim, 2018).
