26.A study investigates whether gender (male or female) influences the likelihood of passing or failing a clinical exam. Participants' genders and their pass/fail outcomes are recorded. Which statistical test is most appropriate to determine if there is a significant association between gender and exam outcome?
Chi-squared test The correct answer is Chi-squared test. The chi-squared test is used to evaluate whether there is a significant association between two categorical variables, in this case, gender (male or female) and clinical exam outcome (pass or fail). Below are the 5 steps leading to this conclusion:

1) Define the research question: The question asks if there is an association between gender and clinical exam outcomes. This indicates a relationship (association) is being tested.
2) Establish the number of groups and whether they are dependent or independent: The two groups (male and female) are independent, as they do not overlap or depend on each other.
3) Identify the variables and their data types: Gender and exam outcome are both categorical variables.
4) Establish if a parametric or non-parametric test is required: Since the data are categorical, parametric tests do not apply. Non-parametric tests, such as the chi-squared test, are used for categorical data.
5) Select the correct statistical test: Based on the above, the chi-squared test is the most appropriate to determine whether there is a statistically significant association between gender and exam outcome.

Paired t-test: The paired t-test is used for comparing means within the same group, typically before and after an intervention. Since this study involves independent groups and categorical data, the paired t-test is not appropriate.

Mann-Whitney test: The Mann-Whitney test compares the distribution of a continuous or ordinal variable between two independent groups. As this study involves categorical variables (gender and exam outcome), this test is not applicable.

Pearson's product-moment coefficient: This test measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables. Gender and exam outcome are categorical, making Pearson's correlation irrelevant in this context.

Unpaired t-test: The unpaired t-test compares the means of a continuous variable between two independent groups. Since the variables in this study are categorical, this test is not suitable.


27.A study is conducted to evaluate the effect of a new medication on blood pressure in a group of participants. Blood pressure readings are taken before and after the medication is started. The data is found to be non-parametric. Which statistical test would be most appropriate to assess whether there is a significant difference in blood pressure before and after the medication?
Wilcoxon signed-rank test The correct answer is Wilcoxon signed-rank test. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is used to compare paired data when the data is non-parametric, such as repeated measures (before and after) in the same participants. This test determines whether there is a significant difference in the median values of the two related samples.

Five-Step Framework:

1) Define the research question: The study aims to determine whether there is a significant difference in blood pressure readings before and after starting a new medication, indicating a focus on differences.
2) Establish the number of groups and whether they are dependent or independent:
There is one group of participants, and the blood pressure readings are repeated measures (dependent), taken from the same individuals before and after the medication.
3) Identify the variables and their data types: The dependent variable is blood pressure, which is continuous. However, the data is explicitly described as non-parametric, meaning it does not meet the assumptions of normal distribution.
4) Establish if parametric or non-parametric tests should be used: Since the data is non-parametric, a non-parametric test is required. The paired nature of the data further narrows down the options to tests suitable for related samples.
5) Select the correct statistical test: Given that the data is paired and non-parametric, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test is the most appropriate test to determine if there is a significant difference in blood pressure before and after the medication.

Explanation of Other Options:

Paired t-test: This test is used for comparing means of two related samples when the data is parametric (normally distributed). However, the question specifies that the data is non-parametric, making this test unsuitable.

Mann-Whitney U test: The Mann-Whitney U test is used to compare two independent groups when the data is non-parametric. In this study, the data is paired (not independent), so this test is inappropriate.

Chi-squared test: The chi-squared test is used for analysing categorical data to assess associations between variables. Blood pressure is a continuous variable, making this test irrelevant.

Unpaired t-test: This test compares the means of two independent groups with parametric data. Since the data in this study is paired and non-parametric, the unpaired t-test is not suitable.


28.A study seeks to determine whether there is a correlation between a patient's weight (in kilograms) and their dosage of prescribed antidepressant medication (in milligrams). Weight is a continuous variable that is normally distributed. Dosage is also a continuous variable but does not meet the assumptions of normal distribution (non-parametric). Which statistical test is most appropriate to evaluate this relationship?
Spearman's rank correlation coefficient The correct answer is Spearman's rank correlation coefficient. This test is appropriate because one of the variables (dosage) is non-parametric. Spearman's rank correlation does not require both variables to be normally distributed and evaluates the strength and direction of a monotonic relationship between two continuous variables.

Explanation with 5 Steps:

1) Define the research question: The study seeks to evaluate whether there is a correlation between a patient's weight (normally distributed) and dosage of prescribed antidepressant medication (non-parametric).
2) Establish how many groups there are and if they are dependent or independent: There are no groups; the analysis involves two continuous variables that are inherently dependent within the same individuals.
3) Identify the variables and data types: Weight: Continuous and normally distributed.
Dosage: Continuous and non-parametric.
4) Establish if parametric or non-parametric tests should be used: As dosage does not meet the assumptions of normality, a non-parametric test is required.
5) Select the correct statistical test: Spearman's rank correlation coefficient is the most appropriate test for these variables, as it handles non-parametric data and measures the correlation between them.

Explanation of Other Options:

Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient: While this test is often used for correlation, it assumes both variables are continuous and normally distributed. As dosage is not normally distributed, this test is inappropriate here.

Chi-squared test: This test evaluates associations between categorical variables. It is unsuitable here as both variables are continuous.

Mann-Whitney U test: This is a non-parametric test used to compare medians between two independent groups. As the study does not involve groups, this test is irrelevant.

Wilcoxon's rank-sum test: This test is used to compare paired data or matched samples for non-parametric variables. It is not suitable for assessing correlation between two continuous variables.

Independent t-test: This test is used to compare the means of two independent groups with normally distributed data. The study involves correlation, not group comparisons, making this test inappropriate.

Paired t-test: This test compares means of paired or matched data from the same sample. Since the research question involves correlation, not paired data, it is not suitable.
