Cognitive tests used in psychiatry


The following cognitive tests are used in psychiatric practice and commonly come up in the MRCPsych exams.

The National Adult Reading Test (NART):

The NART involves reading aloud a list of 50 irregularly spelled words to the test taker. The words are uncommon or have irregular spelling patterns, and the test taker's ability to accurately pronounce these words is evaluated. The accuracy of pronunciation is then used to estimate the individual's pre-morbid IQ level, as reading ability is strongly correlated with cognitive functioning.

The NART is particularly useful in clinical and research settings where an individual's current cognitive abilities may be affected by neurological conditions or brain injuries. By estimating their premorbid IQ level, it provides a baseline to assess the extent of cognitive decline or changes resulting from the condition or injury.

Wechsler Adult Intelligence scale (WAIS):

The WAIS consists of several subtests that assess different cognitive domains, including verbal comprehension, perceptual reasoning, working memory, and processing speed. The subtests cover a wide range of tasks, such as vocabulary knowledge, comprehension, visual-spatial skills, logical reasoning, digit span, and symbol coding. The results of these subtests are combined to generate an overall IQ score, as well as separate index scores that represent specific cognitive abilities.

Raven's Progressive Matrices:


Raven's Progressive Matrices is a non-verbal, culture-fair intelligence test designed to assess abstract reasoning and problem-solving abilities.

The test consists of a series of matrices or patterns, with one piece missing. The task for the test taker is to identify the missing piece from a set of options. The patterns become progressively more complex and challenging as the test progresses.

Raven's Progressive Matrices is considered culture-fair because it does not rely on language or cultural knowledge. The test is designed to minimize the influence of factors such as education, language proficiency, or specific cultural backgrounds, which makes it applicable for a wide range of individuals, regardless of their cultural or linguistic background.

The test measures fluid intelligence, which is the ability to solve novel problems, think abstractly, and adapt to new situations. It provides a measure of an individual's cognitive flexibility, pattern recognition, and logical reasoning skills.

Raven's Progressive Matrices is commonly used in research, educational, and clinical settings. It is particularly valuable when assessing individuals with limited language skills, neurodevelopmental disorders, or those for whom verbal assessments may not be appropriate or reliable.

Rey-Osterrieth Complex Figure:


The test involves presenting the individual with a complex geometric figure and asking them to copy it as accurately as possible. After a short delay, typically ranging from a few minutes to an hour, the individual is then asked to reproduce the figure from memory. Their reproduction is evaluated based on various aspects, such as accuracy, organization, spatial relationships, and inclusion of specific details.

The Rey-Osterrieth Complex Figure provides valuable information about an individual's ability to perceive and reproduce complex visual stimuli, as well as their visual memory and problem-solving skills. It can be used to assess cognitive impairments, such as those resulting from brain injuries, neurodegenerative disorders, or developmental conditions.

Stroop Test:


The Stroop test is a widely used cognitive assessment tool that measures attention, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control. It involves presenting participants with colour words printed in incongruent ink colours (e.g., the word 'red' written in blue ink) and asking them to name the ink colour while ignoring the word itself. The test measures the interference effect, as participants typically experience difficulty overriding the automatic response of reading the word, leading to slower response times and increased errors. The Stroop test is used in research and clinical settings to evaluate executive functions and attentional processing.

Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST):


The Wisconsin Card Sorting Test is a neuropsychological assessment that measures cognitive flexibility, problem-solving, and set-shifting abilities. It involves a deck of cards, each featuring different shapes and colours, and participants are asked to match the cards based on specific rules that change over time. The test evaluates an individual's ability to adapt their thinking and strategy in response to changing rules. Performance is measured by the number of correct and perseverative responses, as well as the ability to learn and apply new rules. The WCST is commonly used in clinical assessments to assess executive functions and identify cognitive impairments related to conditions such as frontal lobe dysfunction or neurodevelopmental disorders.

Trail Making Test:


The Trail Making Test is a neuropsychological assessment tool that primarily assesses cognitive flexibility, visual attention, mental processing speed, and executive functioning. It consists of two parts: Trail Making Test Part A (TMT-A) and Trail Making Test Part B (TMT-B).

In Trail Making Test Part A, the individual is presented with a sheet of paper containing randomly arranged numbers (1-25) distributed across the page. The task is to draw a line connecting the numbers in ascending order as quickly as possible.

In Trail Making Test Part B, the individual is presented with a similar sheet of paper but with both numbers (1-13) and letters (A-L) randomly distributed. The task is to draw lines connecting the numbers and letters in an alternating sequence (1-A-2-B-3-C, and so on) as quickly as possible.

The test measures several cognitive abilities. Trail Making Test Part A primarily assesses visual attention, tracking, and processing speed. It requires the individual to scan and mentally connect the numbers in a specific order. Performance on TMT-A is influenced by factors such as visual perception, attentional control, and psychomotor speed.

Trail Making Test Part B further adds the cognitive components of cognitive flexibility and set-shifting. It requires the individual to switch attention between numbers and letters, shifting mental sets and flexibly adapting their response strategy. Performance on TMT-B is associated with cognitive processes such as mental flexibility, executive functioning, and working memory.

Tower of London:


The Tower of London is a task-based assessment that measures planning, problem-solving, and executive functions. It involves a set of coloured balls arranged in a specific pattern on three pegs, and participants are required to move the balls to match a given target arrangement using the fewest moves possible. The task requires individuals to plan and execute a series of sequential moves while considering the constraints of the task. Performance is measured by the number of moves taken to achieve the target arrangement and the time taken to complete the task. The Tower of London test is used in research and clinical settings to evaluate cognitive abilities, particularly in relation to planning and problem-solving skills.

Continuous Performance Tasks (CPT):

Continuous Performance Tasks are computer-based tests that assess sustained attention, response inhibition, and vigilance. Participants are presented with a series of visual or auditory stimuli and are instructed to respond when specific target stimuli appear while withholding responses for non-target stimuli. The test measures an individual's ability to maintain focus and sustain attention over an extended period, as well as their ability to inhibit impulsive responses. Various parameters, such as reaction time, accuracy, and omission or commission errors, are used to assess performance. CPTs are commonly used in research and clinical assessments to evaluate attentional processes and detect attention-related disorders, such as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

Boston Naming Test:

The Boston Naming Test is a widely used neuropsychological assessment tool that evaluates an individual's ability to name objects or pictures. It consists of a set of black-and-white line drawings of objects, and the individual is asked to verbally provide the correct name for each picture within a specified time limit. The test assesses various aspects of language and semantic memory, including word retrieval, object recognition, and lexical access. It is commonly used in the assessment of language impairments and cognitive disorders, such as aphasia and dementia.

Animal Fluency Test:

The Animal Fluency Test is a verbal fluency task that assesses an individual's ability to generate words within a specific category, in this case, animals. The individual is given a fixed time (usually one minute) and asked to list as many different animal names as possible within that time frame. The test measures semantic fluency, the ability to access and retrieve words from long-term memory, and executive functions involved in word generation and cognitive flexibility. The Animal Fluency Test is commonly used in neuropsychological assessments to evaluate language abilities, frontal lobe functions, and cognitive impairments related to conditions such as dementia or brain injuries.

The Halstead-Reitan battery

The battery consists of a series of standardized tests that evaluate different cognitive domains, including motor speed and coordination, sensory-perceptual functions, attention and concentration, memory, language, and problem-solving abilities. Some of the tests included in the battery are the Category Test, the Tactual Performance Test, the Seashore Rhythm Test, the Trail Making Test, and the Speech Sounds Perception Test, among others.

The Halstead-Reitan battery aims to provide a comprehensive assessment of an individual's cognitive functioning, identify cognitive deficits, and localize potential brain lesions or areas of dysfunction. It is particularly useful in diagnosing and evaluating neurological conditions, such as traumatic brain injury, stroke, brain tumors, and degenerative diseases.
